IRA Basics: Traditional vs. Roth

The two main types of IRAs, Traditional and Roth, primarily differ in how they're taxed. One gives you a potential tax break today, while the other provides for tax-free withdrawals in retirement. Hereâ€™s a side-by-side comparison of their key features.

Traditional IRA

The big idea: Tax break now.

  • ✓
    Contributions: Made with pre-tax dollars, which may be tax-deductible, lowering your current year's tax bill.
  • ✓
    Tax on Growth: Investments grow tax-deferred. You pay no taxes on growth year to year.
  • ✓
    Withdrawals: Taxed as ordinary income in retirement (after age 59 ½).

Roth IRA

The big idea: Tax-free later.

  • ✓
    Contributions: Made with after-tax dollars. There's no upfront tax deduction.
  • ✓
    Tax on Growth: Investments grow completely tax-free.
  • ✓
    Withdrawals: Qualified withdrawals in retirement are 100% tax-free.

Special Rules & Benefits for Savers Over 70

Recent laws have created significant advantages for savers over age 70. Here are the most important changes that provide greater flexibility and new opportunities for tax planning in retirement.

💰

Continue to Contribute

There is no longer an age limit for contributing to a Traditional or Roth IRA, as long as you have earned income. You can contribute up to $8,000 for 2025 (including the $1,000 catch-up).

📅

New RMD Age

The age to begin Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) from a Traditional IRA is now 73. This allows your funds to grow tax-deferred for longer. Roth IRAs have no RMDs for the original owner.

🌟

Charitable Donations (QCDs)

If you are over 70 ½, you can donate up to $105,000 tax-free from your IRA directly to a charity. This powerful strategy can also satisfy your RMD requirement for the year.

General Recommendations & Strategies

1. Determine Your Eligibility First

Estimate your total 2025 income to confirm you can deduct a Traditional IRA contribution or contribute to a Roth IRA. Deductibility can be limited if you have a workplace retirement plan.

2. Time Your Contribution

You have until the tax filing deadline (typically April 15 of the following year) to make your IRA contribution for a given tax year, giving you time to assess your full financial picture.

3. Prioritize Workplace Plans with a Match

If an employer offers a 401(k) with a matching contribution, it's often wise to contribute enough to get the full match before funding an IRA. An employer match is a 100% return on investment.

4. Consult a Professional

A financial advisor or tax professional can provide personalized advice based on your complete financial picture and help you navigate the rules for your specific situation.
